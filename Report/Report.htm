Introduction

Some of the early methods of sound synthesis were additive, wavetable, and FM synthesis, all of which were not particularly computationally intensive but all sounded rather artificial, due to the fact that the spectral content of the sounds was relatively constant or predictable. The paradigm of Physical Modelling came out of the desire to produce sounds that were more interesting and took inspiration from real musical instruments, as well as having the ability to produce abstract virtual instruments that had the satisfying morphing qualities of their real counterparts. Acoustic physical models have various parameters that are linked to the physical nature of an object, such as elasticity or size, which change how they respond to inputs. Compared to other methods of sound synthesis, one of the most notable differences is the naturalness of the sounds produced, making them an intriguing field of research in creating novel virtual musical instruments.

Pros and Cons

Physical models are superior in their ability to generate natural sounds to other forms of synthesis, such as wavetable which gets very boring as the samples never change, and are merely repeated. Relatively few parameters are needed to alter the sound and the models are generally perceptually easier to control, unlike additive synthesisers which require large numbers of sinusoids, amplitudes and phases, the calculations of which are not practical even for the most experienced engineer or musician. Consequently, these models allow minor changes to be made with minimal effort, so modelled instruments could be beneficial in recording, as changes to notes dont require a whole rerecording.

However, there are some downfalls that mainly revolve around the fact that physical models can be much more computationally expensive than their counterparts. For instance, the number of timbres that can be produced using an FM scheme is huge when compared to the computation required to perform the synthesis. This means that real-time applications are much harder, or impossible, to realise with current computing power. With simulations of some complex models taking days to render an output, this greatly reduces the feasibility of these models to be used as live instruments or for composition [8].

Capability of the System

The system is capable of simulating any number of masses with any combination of spring constants and damping factors for as long as desired. However, there are some constraints that must be adhered by for the system to function properly or at all:

·         The number of masses must be 3 or more due to the way the loop is set up that calculates the next displacements of the masses

·         The damping factors  must be positive else the system blows up

·         The spring constants  must be positive else the output is undefined

Future Development

The ability to make the system fully general in terms of being able to handle one and two mass networks would be desirable.

Currently if any invalid values are input they are not caught and so just break the system, so a way of militating against this would be useful.

Additional effects and enhancements, or a more refined reverberation model would greatly increase the quality of the sounds produced and give more scope for using the system as a musical aid.

Expanding to two dimensions would allow the system to simulate membranes as well as just strings, hugely expanding its potential for audio synthesis.
