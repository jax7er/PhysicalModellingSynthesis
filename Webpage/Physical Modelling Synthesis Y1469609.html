Introduction

Physical and related models for sounds design and procedural audio systems

Physical Modelling a paradigm of sound synthesis and has been of interest in the research environment since it's emergence in the 1980's [1]. It concerns all aspects of taking a physical system, real or abstract, and describing it using physical principles to then be simplified into a discrete numerical system.

Any object in the real world can have a physical model associated with it, as they all obey a certain set of physical laws. For example, a string can be considered a chain of coupled nodes, each of which is connected to it's neighbour and exerts a force linearly related to it's relative displacement and velocity.

Key Points and Types

Physical models vary from other forms of sound synthesis in a number of ways. Firstly, the way they are designed is quite neat, with the sound generation and control aspects of the system being separated. This means that the sample rates for the sound generation and the control signals need not be the same.

The sounds generated from these models, can easily be compared to real life instruments, as they have a natural quality to them. The sounds are tangible and familiar as they represent those which we hear in everyday life and morph and develop much like real sounds do.
